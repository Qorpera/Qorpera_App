# Human-AI Trust Calibration: Research Foundations for Graduated Autonomy Systems

**Researched:** 2026-03-20
**Prompt:** Research academic and industry work on human-AI trust calibration — the overtrust/undertrust problem, UI patterns for trust calibration, graduated autonomy models, transparency effects, automation complacency prevention, and case studies — to provide research backing for Qorpera's Observe → Propose → Act trust gradient.

## Key Findings

- **Qorpera's three-stage model (Observe → Propose → Act) maps precisely to the now-mainstream enterprise deployment pattern** (Alert → Decision → Action) validated by AWS, McKinsey, Deloitte, and 30 years of human factors research showing intermediate automation levels outperform both full automation and manual control (Endsley & Kiris, 1995).
- **The single biggest design risk is rubber-stamping at the Propose stage.** Cognitive forcing functions — requiring users to form independent judgments before seeing AI output — are the most research-validated countermeasure, significantly outperforming explanation-only approaches (Bucinca et al., 2021, N=199). Track approval patterns: if a user approves 100% of proposals in under 5 seconds, flag for complacency.
- **Transparency has an inverted U-shaped relationship with trust.** Moderate, targeted explanations (2-3 key signals) yield 81-87% task performance vs. 55% for comprehensive reasoning dumps. Critically, Bansal et al. (CHI 2021) found explanations increase acceptance regardless of correctness — meaning persuasive reasoning chains can make wrong answers look right.
- **Per-situation-type autonomy graduation is the correct granularity.** The Earned Autonomy Gradient (Roder, 2025) explicitly states the same agent should receive different autonomy levels for different task classes. Failure disposition (how the AI behaves when wrong) is more important than raw accuracy for graduation decisions — confident confabulation ranks as more dangerous than simply missing a problem.
- **Trust builds slowly and breaks quickly.** KPMG's 2025 global study (48,000+ respondents, 47 countries) found 66% use AI without evaluating accuracy yet only 46% are willing to trust it. Trust has decreased since 2022 even as adoption increased. Post-error trust repair requires immediate causal attribution ("why the error occurred") combined with boundary specification ("system limitations").

## Full Research

### 1. The Overtrust/Undertrust Problem

#### Core Concepts

Trust calibration in AI is defined as the alignment between a human user's subjective trust and the system's objective trustworthiness. Well-calibrated trust occurs when user confidence matches the actual performance of an AI system, minimizing both overtrust (leading to misuse) and undertrust (leading to disuse).

Parasuraman & Riley (1997) established the foundational taxonomy:
- **Use**: Voluntary activation or disengagement of automation, influenced by trust, workload, and risk
- **Misuse**: Overreliance on automation, resulting in monitoring failures or decision biases
- **Disuse**: Neglect or underutilization of automation, commonly caused by false alarms or perceived unreliability
- **Abuse**: Automation of functions without due regard for consequences on human performance

#### Key Quantitative Findings

**KPMG/University of Melbourne Global Study (2025)** — surveyed 48,000+ people across 47 countries:
- 66% of people use AI regularly; 83% believe AI will deliver wide-ranging benefits
- Yet only 46% are willing to trust AI systems
- 66% rely on AI output without evaluating accuracy
- 56% report making mistakes in their work due to AI
- Trust has *decreased* since pre-ChatGPT 2022, even as adoption has increased

**Overreliance in Practice:**
- 68.9% of students exhibited increased laziness from AI dialogue system overreliance; 27.7% experienced degradation in decision-making abilities (systematic review, 2024)
- Mere knowledge that advice was generated by AI causes people to overrely, even when it contradicts available contextual information (behavioral experiment, 2024)
- Overreliance increases as tasks get harder; explanations reduce overreliance only for hard tasks, not easy ones (Vasconcelos et al., 2023; across 5 experiments, N=731)

**Miscalibrated AI Confidence (Li et al., 2024):**
- Users relied more on overconfident AI and tended to over-rely on it
- Users relied less on underconfident AI and exhibited under-reliance
- AI miscalibration is difficult for users to detect without explicit calibration communication
- Communicating calibration levels helps detection but decreases trust, leading to high under-reliance — a lose-lose without careful design

**Dzindolet, Peterson, Pomranky, Pierce & Beck (2003):**
- Participants initially trusted automated decision aids highly
- After observing errors, participants distrusted even reliable aids — unless an explanation was provided for why errors might occur
- Knowing why the aid might err increased trust and reliance, even when that trust was unwarranted
- Uninformed operators developed unrealistic expectations; informed operators developed more appropriate trust levels

#### Design Implications for Qorpera

1. **The trust-adoption gap is real and quantified.** Qorpera's graduated model (Observe → Propose → Act) directly addresses the 46% willing-to-trust vs. 66% using-without-evaluating gap by forcing engagement at the Propose stage before autonomous action.
2. **Err toward undertrust initially.** The data shows overtrust is harder to correct and more dangerous than undertrust. Starting all situation types at "Observe" and requiring demonstrated accuracy before graduation is research-validated.
3. **Explain why errors happen, not just that they happened.** Dzindolet et al.'s finding that error explanations increase appropriate trust (even sometimes excessively) supports Qorpera's evidence-based reasoning output — showing *why* the AI reached a conclusion and *why* it might be wrong.
4. **Monitor for the "66% problem."** Over half of users accept AI output without evaluation. Qorpera's approval workflow at the Propose stage is a critical intervention point, but the UI must make approval an active cognitive task, not a rubber stamp.

### 2. UI Patterns for Trust Calibration

#### Confidence Visualization Patterns

The Confidence Visualization Patterns (CVP) framework from Agentic Design identifies specific UI approaches:

**Color-coded confidence system:**
- Green: High confidence (95%+)
- Orange: Medium confidence (~70%)
- Red: Low confidence (~30%)

**Key principles:**
- Show not just what AI thinks, but how confident it is — and explain what drives that confidence
- Display uncertainty ranges rather than single point estimates
- Use simplified scales (Low/Medium/High) rather than false-precision percentages (e.g., avoid "99.73%" in favor of "very high")

**When to show confidence:**
- High-stakes decision-making scenarios
- Predictions with varying certainty levels
- Multiple competing interpretations
- NOT for simple confirmatory tasks or deterministic rule-based outputs

#### Trust Calibration Design Patterns (AI UX Design Guide)

Seven core principles for progressive trust building:

1. **Explicit opt-in for autonomy**: Never increase autonomy without asking — even after 100% accuracy
2. **Visible confidence levels**: "Very confident" vs. "guessing here" changes user behavior
3. **Corrective learning signals**: After errors, communicate adjustments — "I made an error with X. Here's what I'll do differently"
4. **Power user dashboards**: Per-domain accuracy, error logs, escalation history
5. **Milestone celebration**: "100 tasks completed with 97% accuracy" reinforces appropriate trust
6. **Domain-specific calibration**: Track trust separately by task type (the AI may excel at one situation type and struggle with another)
7. **Asymmetric trust dynamics**: Trust builds slowly and breaks quickly — a single visible failure should trigger proportional, not total, trust reduction

#### Track Record Displays

Research supports showing AI performance history to users:
- Horizontal bar charts for per-domain accuracy comparison
- Celebratory but subtle milestone cards
- Inline autonomy upgrade suggestions within domain rows
- Traffic light system (green/amber/red) for quick trust assessment
- Expandable error log showing mistakes and corrections
- Trend arrows indicating performance trajectory

#### Google PAIR Guidebook Recommendations

Google's People + AI Research team identifies four confidence display approaches:
1. **Categorical**: Bucket into High/Medium/Low with clear associated actions
2. **N-best alternatives**: Show multiple likely predictions rather than explicit scores
3. **Numeric percentages**: Risky — assumes users understand probability; requires substantial domain context
4. **Data visualizations**: Error bars or shaded ranges for specialized domains

**When NOT to show confidence:**
- It won't materially impact user decisions
- It could mislead less-technical users
- System behavior already matches user expectations

#### Trust-Building Stages (PAIR Framework)

| Stage | Focus | Tactics |
|-------|-------|---------|
| Establishment | Initial onboarding | Communicate capabilities and limitations early; reference trusted sources |
| Growth | Early use | Provide low-commitment sandbox experiences; enable preferences and feedback |
| Maintenance | Ongoing use | Progressively increase automation; remind users of permission contexts |
| Recovery | After errors | Communicate recovery plans proactively; let users shift to manual control |

#### Salesforce Trust Design Patterns (Production Implementation)

Salesforce implements five categories of trust patterns in Einstein/Agentforce:

1. **Mindful Friction**: Intentional pauses for human review at critical decision points
2. **Awareness of AI**: Visual indicators (sparkles icon) when content originates from AI
3. **Bias & Toxicity Safeguards**: Guardrails preventing harmful outputs
4. **Explainability & Accuracy**: Citations allowing source validation; redesigned for readability
5. **Hallucination Reduction**: Prompt constraints limiting generation scope

Key statistic: **63% of global workers say human oversight would build their trust in AI** (Salesforce research).

#### Design Implications for Qorpera

1. **Domain-specific trust tracking is essential.** Qorpera already tracks accuracy per situation type — this should be surfaced to users as a trust dashboard showing per-type accuracy, error history, and performance trends.
2. **Explicit opt-in for graduation.** When a situation type reaches the accuracy threshold for Propose → Act graduation, present it as a user decision with supporting data, not an automatic promotion.
3. **Show evidence at every stage.** The reasoning engine's evidence-based analysis should be displayed with the situation card — not hidden behind a click. For the Propose stage, the evidence IS the trust-building mechanism.
4. **Avoid false precision.** When showing confidence, use categorical labels ("high confidence based on 3 corroborating signals") rather than percentages that imply unwarranted precision.
5. **Mindful friction at Propose stage.** Approval actions should require a deliberate cognitive step — not a single "Approve" button but something that requires engaging with the reasoning (e.g., "I've reviewed the evidence" checkbox before approve).
6. **Celebrate milestones visibly.** "This situation type has been correctly identified 50 times with 96% accuracy" gives users concrete grounds for trust progression.

### 3. Graduated Autonomy Models

#### Five Levels of AI Agent Autonomy (Knight First Amendment Institute)

| Level | Human Role | Description |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| 1 | **Operator** | Full control; agent provides on-demand support |
| 2 | **Collaborator** | Close partnership with frequent communication |
| 3 | **Consultant** | Agent takes initiative; user provides directional guidance |
| 4 | **Approver** | Agent operates independently; user approves consequential actions |
| 5 | **Observer** | Monitoring only via activity logs; fully autonomous |

Key insight: **Autonomy operates as an independent design variable from capability.** An agent can be highly capable but deliberately constrained to a lower autonomy level. Developers can calibrate user involvement intentionally regardless of the system's technical ability.

Risk at Level 4: **"Rubber stamping" disengagement** — when users stop meaningfully reviewing approvals.

The framework proposes **Autonomy Certificates**: third-party certifications specifying maximum operational autonomy levels, requiring demonstrated behavioral alignment, with renewal when specifications change.

#### The Earned Autonomy Gradient (Roder, 2025)

This framework turns "should we trust AI?" into an operational question: "Has this agent earned the next increment of autonomy, for this class of task, based on observed behavior?"

**Five-level failure disposition scale:**

| Behavior at Failure Boundary | Score (delta) | Autonomy Level | Required Action |
|----|-----|-----|-----|
| Identifies gap, self-corrects | 1.0 | Full autonomy | None |
| Identifies gap, explains correctly | 0.85 | Autonomous + audit | Monitoring |
| Identifies gap, flags honestly | 0.7 | Recommend & confirm | Human approval |
| Misses the gap entirely | 0.3 | Active oversight | Continuous supervision |
| Identifies gap, invents confident false narrative | 0.1 | Dangerous | Restrict immediately |

**Critical design principle: The scale is deliberately non-monotonic.** Confident confabulation (delta=0.1) ranks below failing to notice problems (delta=0.3) because invented explanations "terminate human investigation" and prevent error correction.

**Trust variables combine three vectors gated by delta:**
- Task risk: Reversibility and stakes
- Agent competence: Domain-specific capability
- Environmental safeguards: Available guardrails

All three are recursively dependent on delta — poor failure disposition undermines other positive factors.

**Implementation characteristics:**
- **Earned**: Demonstrated through behavior, not benchmarks
- **Contextual**: Same agent receives different autonomy levels for different task classes
- **Measurable**: Observable inputs producing testable outputs
- **Revocable**: Autonomy automatically contracts if delta degrades

#### Staged Enterprise Deployment (Industry Practice, 2025-2026)

AWS, McKinsey, and Deloitte converge on staged autonomy approaches:

**Stage 1 — Autonomously Alerting**: Proactive detection; human reviews all outputs
**Stage 2 — Autonomously Decisioning**: Goal-aligned choices within parameters; human review at checkpoints
**Stage 3 — Autonomously Actioning**: Executing changes under policy guardrails and audit trails

McKinsey recommends: "Design for trust first, speed second" and "Start with bounded autonomy, but make sure you're keeping humans accountable for high-impact decisions and scale only when the monitoring shows the system behaves predictably."

Gartner predicts 40% of enterprise software will feature task-specific AI agents by end of 2026, but most will operate at Levels 1-2 initially.

#### Design Implications for Qorpera

1. **Qorpera's Observe → Propose → Act maps precisely to the industry-standard three-stage model** (Alert → Decision → Action). This is now mainstream enterprise thinking, validating the core architecture.
2. **Per-situation-type autonomy is the correct granularity.** The Earned Autonomy Gradient explicitly states that the same agent should receive different autonomy levels for different task classes. Qorpera's per-SituationType graduation is exactly this pattern.
3. **Monitor failure behavior, not just accuracy.** The delta metric (failure disposition) is potentially more important than raw accuracy. When Qorpera's AI misidentifies a situation, does it explain the uncertainty, or does it confabulate a confident-sounding rationale? This distinction matters for graduation decisions.
4. **Revocability is essential.** Autonomy must contract automatically when performance degrades. Qorpera's autonomy graduation logic should include automatic demotion thresholds, not just graduation thresholds.
5. **The "rubber stamping" risk at Level 4 (Approver)** is the biggest danger for Qorpera's Propose stage. Users who routinely approve without reviewing essentially make Propose equivalent to Act without the safeguards. Anti-rubber-stamping mechanisms are critical.
6. **Earned, not assumed.** The research explicitly rejects benchmark-based trust ("the model scored 95% on our test set") in favor of behavior-based trust. Qorpera should graduate based on production accuracy on real situations, not lab performance.

### 4. Transparency and Trust Calibration

#### The Transparency-Trust Relationship

**Core finding:** Increased transparency about the reasoning behind automated advice increases the accuracy of automation use (Taylor & Francis, 2025). However, this relationship is far from simple.

#### The Transparency Paradox

Recent research (2025-2026) reveals a critical non-linear relationship:

**Inverted U-shaped effect**: Moderate transparency fosters trust and certainty, but excessive transparency leads to cognitive overload and heightened scrutiny, reducing AI adoption (Ngo, 2025).

**Quantitative evidence from healthcare:**
- Global explanations (comprehensive reasoning): High mental effort, 55% performance
- Local explanations (specific, targeted reasoning): Minimal effort, 81-87% performance
- The critical threshold aligns with observations that cognitive benefits plateau around two explanation dimensions

**Autonomy Depletion Framework (2026):** Users disengage because cognitive overload undermines their sense of control, not primarily because they distrust the AI. Information provision triggers metacognitive processing which depletes cognitive resources and reduces perceived control when load exceeds working memory capacity.

#### Types of Explanations and Their Effects

**Bansal, Nushi, Kamar et al. (CHI 2021)** — "Does the Whole Exceed its Parts?":
- Studied whether explanations improve complementary team performance (human + AI > either alone)
- Key finding: **Explanations increased the chance that humans accept AI recommendations regardless of correctness** — they did not improve discrimination between correct and incorrect AI outputs
- This directly challenges the assumption that more explanation = better decisions

**Zhang, Liao & Bellamy (FAccT 2020):**
- Confidence scores can help calibrate trust
- But local, prediction-specific explanations may not create perceivable effects for trust calibration
- Trust calibration alone is insufficient to improve AI-assisted decision making — the human must bring complementary knowledge

**Cognitive Forcing Functions (Bucinca, Malaya & Gajos, 2021):**
- N=199 participants across three cognitive forcing designs vs. two explanation approaches vs. no-AI baseline
- Cognitive forcing (requiring users to form their own judgment before seeing AI's) significantly reduced overreliance compared to simple explainable AI approaches
- People rarely engage analytically with individual AI recommendations; they develop general heuristics about whether to follow suggestions
- Dual-process theory: Users default to System 1 (fast, heuristic) thinking; interventions must trigger System 2 (analytical) engagement

#### Interactive Explanations

**Counterfactual explanations** achieved the highest trust alignment:
- Correlation rho=0.82 with lowest calibration error CE=0.06 in loan approval simulations (Sunny, 2025)
- Static feature-importance charts produced moderate improvements but risked overtrust with less accurate models

**Dynamic trust calibration** using contextual bandits showed 10-38% increases in task rewards and consistent trust misalignment reductions (Henrique et al., 2025).

#### Trust Repair After Errors

**Trust Recovery Journey (IUI 2024):**
- Trust significantly decreases after AI errors but can be rapidly restored
- Timing of explanatory interventions is crucial — post-error explanations delivered immediately following failures are most effective
- Explanations combining causal attribution (why the error occurred) with boundary specification (system limitations) prove most effective for competence-based trust repair

**Key principle: Trust builds slowly and breaks quickly.** A single visible failure should trigger proportional, not total, trust reduction. Repetitive apologies lose effectiveness; corrections must feel authentic and substantive.

#### Self-Assessment and Uncertainty Communication

**Frontiers in Robotics and AI (2025):** Systems with self-assessment capabilities showed approximately 40% improvement in human trust through closed-loop trust calibration. Machines must perform actions to calibrate human trust based on an accurate assessment of both their capability and human trust in real time.

#### Design Implications for Qorpera

1. **Optimize for local, targeted explanations.** The transparency paradox data strongly suggests Qorpera's reasoning output should be concise and focused — "Here are the 2-3 key signals that triggered this situation" rather than a comprehensive dump of all context. The 81-87% vs. 55% performance gap is dramatic.
2. **Implement cognitive forcing at the Propose stage.** Before showing the AI's recommended action, consider asking users to briefly state what they think should happen. This triggers System 2 thinking and dramatically reduces rubber-stamp approval.
3. **Explanation format matters more than explanation presence.** Bansal et al.'s finding that explanations increase acceptance regardless of correctness is a warning. Qorpera's explanations should be designed to help users *discriminate* between good and bad AI judgments, not just feel more confident.
4. **Build trust repair into the graduation system.** When an AI makes an error at the Propose stage and the user rejects the proposal, the system should immediately explain what happened and what it will do differently — this is the causal attribution + boundary specification pattern that research shows is most effective.
5. **Self-assessment capability is a differentiator.** The 40% trust improvement from machine self-assessment supports building explicit uncertainty indicators into Qorpera's situation cards. When the AI is uncertain, it should say so clearly.
6. **Avoid the Bansal trap.** If Qorpera shows a persuasive reasoning chain AND a recommended action, users may accept the action because the reasoning looks good — even when it's wrong. Consider separating the "here's what I found" (evidence) from the "here's what I recommend" (action) to encourage independent evaluation.

### 5. Automation Complacency

#### The Problem

Automation complacency is the tendency for human operators to become over-reliant on automated systems, reducing their vigilance and failing to detect automation errors. It is distinct from but related to automation bias (the tendency to favor automated suggestions over contradictory information from other sources).

#### The Out-of-the-Loop (OOTL) Performance Problem

Defined by Endsley & Kiris (1995), the OOTL problem manifests in four ways:
1. **Vigilance decrements**: Operator failure to observe parameter changes and intervene when necessary
2. **Complacency**: Over-trust in automated controllers
3. **Loss of situation awareness**: Operator loses understanding of system/situation state
4. **Skill decay**: Operator loses ability to perform tasks manually

Key finding: The OOTL problem was significantly greater under full automation than under intermediate levels. By implementing functions at lower automation levels — leaving the operator involved in active decision-making — situation awareness remained higher and subjects were more able to assume manual control when needed.

#### Complacency in Modern AI Systems

**Healthcare (2025):** The attentional integration model proposes that reallocation of attentional resources away from decision-making results in decreased situational awareness and learned complacency. When clinicians become complacent and direct their attention elsewhere, there may be insufficient monitoring of automation output.

**Security Operations (2024):** Increased automation in security operations centers amplifies automation bias risk, with analysts becoming over-reliant on automation and failing to seek confirmatory or contradictory information.

**Education (2024):** Students receiving direct AI answers showed higher reliance compared to those receiving instructions, and initial reliance on direct answers impaired performance on subsequent tasks when the AI erred.

#### Prevention Strategies

**Cognitive Forcing Functions (Bucinca et al., 2021):**
- Require users to form their own judgment before seeing AI recommendations
- Allow users to control when they receive AI assistance
- Slow down the decision-making process at critical junctures
- These interventions significantly outperform explanation-only approaches

**Human Confirmation Checkpoints:**
- System pauses at important moments and requests human confirmation before proceeding
- Ensures users stay mentally present and take ownership of decisions
- Must require genuine engagement, not just clicking "OK"

**Domain Expertise and Training:**
- Domain expertise and advanced background knowledge are the most protective factors against automation bias
- Verification-related cognitive engagement serves as a critical debiasing mechanism

**Organizational Approaches:**
- Institutional arrangements emphasizing human judgment
- Providing transparency about algorithmic limitations
- Rewarding critical engagement with system outputs
- Varied automation levels to maintain operator engagement

**Intermediate Levels of Automation (Endsley):**
- Rather than full automation or full manual control, intermediate levels that keep the human actively involved produce the best outcomes
- This directly supports the Observe → Propose → Act gradient over binary automated/manual approaches

#### Design Implications for Qorpera

1. **The Propose stage is Qorpera's primary anti-complacency mechanism.** Research strongly validates that intermediate automation (where the human remains in the decision loop) outperforms both full automation and manual operation. The Propose stage is exactly this intermediate level.
2. **Design against rubber-stamping.** The biggest complacency risk is users who routinely approve Propose recommendations without review. Countermeasures:
   - Vary the approval interface to prevent habitual clicking
   - Occasionally present situations where the AI explicitly expresses uncertainty and asks for human judgment
   - Track approval patterns — if a user approves 100% of proposals in under 5 seconds, flag for potential complacency
   - Require engagement with evidence before showing the "Approve" button
3. **Periodic manual engagement.** Even at the Act stage (full autonomy), periodically present situations for human review to maintain domain awareness. Research on intermediate automation levels supports this pattern.
4. **Demotion as a safety valve.** If user engagement drops below a threshold (rapid approvals, no evidence review), consider automatically demoting the situation type back to Observe to force re-engagement.
5. **Train users on AI failure modes.** Dzindolet et al.'s finding that explaining *why* the AI might err improves trust calibration suggests that Qorpera's onboarding should include examples of when and how the AI gets things wrong, not just when it succeeds.
6. **Never let Act become invisible.** Even autonomous actions should be logged with full reasoning and surfaced in a dashboard. The OOTL problem is worst when automation is invisible. Make autonomous actions visible without requiring action — this keeps users in a monitoring role even at Level 5.

### 6. Case Studies

#### Tesla Autopilot — Overtrust Through Naming

**What happened:** Tesla's Autopilot system, despite being a Level 2 driver assistance system, was involved in multiple fatal crashes where drivers failed to maintain attention.

**Trust problem:** The name "Autopilot" was misleading, contributing to initial overtrust. Even participants who developed correct mental models of the automation's capabilities and limitations still misused the system. Drivers self-reported: *"I will raise my hand and say 'I over-trust Autopilot'. I use it too liberally."*

**Key finding:** A correct understanding of system limitations is necessary but NOT sufficient to prevent misuse. Overtrust can persist even with accurate mental models, because complacency develops through routine safe operation.

**Lesson for Qorpera:** Naming and framing matter enormously. The "Observe → Propose → Act" language is clear about what each level means. Avoid language that implies more autonomy than exists (e.g., don't call Propose "auto-suggest" or Act "autopilot").

#### Boeing 737 MAX MCAS — Automation Abuse and Hidden Autonomy

**What happened:** Boeing's MCAS (Maneuvering Characteristics Augmentation System) relied on a single angle-of-attack sensor. Erroneous readings caused the system to pitch the nose down, leading to two crashes killing 346 people.

**Trust problems:**
- Pilots were not adequately informed about MCAS or its authority over the aircraft
- The system had hidden autonomy — it could override pilot inputs without clear indication
- No redundancy: single sensor feeding an autonomous system
- This represents Parasuraman & Riley's "automation abuse" — automating functions without regard for human performance consequences

**Lesson for Qorpera:** Never grant autonomy without full transparency about what the system is doing and why. At the Act level, every autonomous action must be visible, explainable, and reversible. Users must always understand the system's authority boundaries.

#### IBM Watson for Oncology — Overpromise and Black-Box Trust Failure

**What happened:** IBM invested ~$4 billion in Watson for Oncology, promising AI-driven cancer treatment recommendations. MD Anderson Cancer Center cancelled their partnership after spending $62 million when the system recommended "unsafe and incorrect" treatments.

**Trust problems:**
- **Overpromised capabilities**: Aggressive marketing created unrealistic expectations
- **Training data issues**: Trained on synthetic/hypothetical cases, not real patient data; recommendations reflected 1-2 physicians' preferences rather than evidence-based medicine
- **Black-box reasoning**: Physicians could not understand or verify Watson's recommendations
- **No context awareness**: Treatment recommendations from Manhattan hospitals didn't translate to different healthcare systems worldwide
- **Workflow disruption**: Interface was not user-friendly; limited end-user involvement during development

**Lesson for Qorpera:** (a) Never overpromise AI capabilities — set realistic expectations during onboarding. (b) Show reasoning transparently so users can verify. (c) Ground recommendations in actual operational data, not synthetic scenarios. (d) Build with end-users from the start, not just for them.

#### Salesforce Einstein/Agentforce — Trust Patterns in Production

**What they do well:**
- **Mindful friction**: Intentional pauses at critical decision points
- **Visual AI indicators**: Sparkles icon consistently marks AI-generated content
- **Citations**: Users can trace recommendations back to source data
- **Multi-channel feedback**: Edits, hover-overs, explicit feedback, thumbs up/down
- **Trust Layer architecture**: Data masking, zero-retention, LLM audit trail

**Key insight:** Salesforce treats trust design as a first-class product concern with dedicated pattern categories, not an afterthought. Their finding that 63% of workers say human oversight would build their trust directly validates human-in-the-loop approaches.

**Lesson for Qorpera:** Implement trust as a product feature, not a compliance checkbox. Specific patterns like mindful friction, source citations, and multi-channel feedback loops are proven in production at scale.

#### GitHub Copilot — Progressive Trust Through Acceptance Patterns

**What they do well:**
- Suggestions start conservative (well-established patterns)
- Progressively become more complex and opinionated as user acceptance increases
- System adapts to individual user's acceptance patterns over time
- Rejection triggers shorter, less frequent suggestions

**Lesson for Qorpera:** Individual adaptation to acceptance patterns is a powerful trust calibration mechanism. If a user consistently rejects a certain category of proposals, reduce confidence in that area. If they consistently accept, it's a signal (but not proof) that graduation may be appropriate.

#### Consumer Reports AskCR — Trust Through Grounding

**What they do well:**
- RAG system answers questions using only CR's trusted archives
- Eliminates hallucination risk by constraining to verified sources
- Trust derives from the quality of the underlying data, not the AI's reasoning

**Lesson for Qorpera:** Qorpera's content pipeline (connector sync → content chunks → pgvector retrieval) already implements this pattern. Making the data provenance visible to users (e.g., "based on 3 Slack messages and 1 HubSpot deal update") turns the content pipeline into a trust feature.

### 7. Key Papers and Frameworks

#### Foundational Frameworks

**Lee & See (2004) — Trust in Automation: Designing for Appropriate Reliance**
*Human Factors, 46(1), 50-80*

The single most-cited framework for trust in automation. Key contributions:
- **Definition**: Trust is "the attitude that an agent will help achieve an individual's goals in a situation characterized by uncertainty and vulnerability"
- **Three bases of trust**: Performance (what the system does), Process (how it operates), Purpose (why it was designed that way)
- **Dynamic feedback loop**: Trust influences reliance behavior; reliance behavior produces experiences that update trust
- **Three cognitive processes**: Analytic (rational evaluation), Analogical (comparison to past experiences), Affective (emotional responses to violations or confirmations)
- **Key insight**: Because people respond to technology socially, trust guides reliance especially when complexity makes complete understanding impractical

**Parasuraman & Riley (1997) — Humans and Automation: Use, Misuse, Disuse, Abuse**
*Human Factors, 39(2), 230-253*

Established the four-category taxonomy of automation interaction failures. Over 3,500 citations. The framework demonstrates that trust failures aren't just about the technology — they're about the relationship between humans and automation, including organizational and design choices (abuse) that set the conditions for misuse and disuse.

**Hoff & Bashir (2015) — Trust in Automation: Integrating Empirical Evidence**
*Human Factors, 57(3), 407-434*

Systematic review of 127 studies (2002-2013). Identified three layers of trust variability:
1. **Dispositional trust**: Individual differences in propensity to trust technology
2. **Situational trust**: Context-dependent factors (workload, risk, organizational culture)
3. **Learned trust**: Experience-based trust from direct interaction with the system

Three primary sources of trust influence: automation characteristics, user characteristics, and environmental context.

**Chiou & Lee (2023) — Trusting Automation: Designing for Responsivity and Resilience**
*Human Factors, 65(1), 137-165*

An important update to the Lee & See framework for modern AI systems. Key reframing:
- The goal is NOT to maximize trust, or even to calibrate trust, but to **support a process of trusting through automation responsivity**
- Four-concept model: Situation, Semiotics, Strategy, Sequence
- In modern systems where humans shift from operators to co-operators, **responsivity and the ability to resolve conflicting goals may be more relevant than reliability and reliance**
- This shift from "calibrated trust" to "ongoing trusting relationship" is significant for systems like Qorpera that evolve over time

#### Recent Key Papers (2023-2026)

**Bucinca, Malaya & Gajos (2021) — To Trust or to Think**
*Proc. ACM Hum.-Comput. Interact., 5(CSCW1), N=199*

Demonstrated that cognitive forcing functions (requiring independent judgment before seeing AI output) significantly reduce overreliance, outperforming explanation-based approaches. Grounded in dual-process theory — most users default to System 1 heuristic thinking with AI, and interventions must trigger System 2 analytical engagement.

**Bansal, Wu, Zhou, Fok, Nushi, Kamar, Ribeiro & Weld (CHI 2021) — Does the Whole Exceed Its Parts?**
*Mixed-method user studies on three datasets*

Found that AI explanations increased acceptance of AI recommendations regardless of correctness. Complementary performance (human+AI > either alone) was observed but not increased by explanations. Poses fundamental challenges for XAI design.

**Zhang, Liao & Bellamy (FAccT 2020) — Effect of Confidence and Explanation on Accuracy and Trust Calibration**

Confidence scores help calibrate trust, but local prediction-specific explanations may not create perceivable calibration effects. Trust calibration alone is insufficient — the human must bring complementary knowledge.

**Li et al. (2024) — Understanding Effects of Miscalibrated AI Confidence**

Demonstrated that users cannot detect miscalibration without explicit communication; overconfident AI causes over-reliance; underconfident AI causes under-reliance. Even communicating miscalibration doesn't fully solve the problem because it decreases general trust.

**Vasconcelos et al. (2023) — Explanations Can Reduce Overreliance on AI Systems During Decision-Making**
*5 experiments, N=731*

Established that overreliance increases with task difficulty; explanations reduce overreliance for hard tasks but not easy ones; costs (task difficulty, explanation difficulty) and benefits (compensation) both affect overreliance.

**Ngo (2025) — Balancing AI Transparency: Trust, Certainty, and Adoption**

Documented the inverted U-shaped relationship between transparency and trust: moderate transparency optimal, excessive transparency causes cognitive overload and reduces adoption.

**Transparency Paradox (2026) — Autonomy Depletion Through Cognitive Load**

Proposed that users disengage from over-explained AI not because of distrust but because cognitive overload undermines their sense of control. Healthcare study: global explanations (55% performance) vs. local explanations (81-87% performance).

**Steinmetz et al. (2025) — Trust Calibration Maturity Model (TCMM)**

Scores AI systems across five dimensions on a 1-4 scale:
1. Performance Characterization
2. Bias & Robustness Quantification
3. Transparency
4. Safety & Security
5. Usability

**Larasati (2025) — Human-AI Trust Attitude Scale (HATAS)**

Eight-dimension psychometric instrument (Cronbach's alpha ~0.95): Reliability, Technical Competence, Helpfulness, Understandability, Faith, Personal Attachment, User Autonomy, Institutional Credibility.

**Roder (2025) — The Earned Autonomy Gradient**

Framework measuring trust through failure disposition (delta) rather than accuracy. Five-level scale from dangerous confabulation (0.1) to self-correcting (1.0). Key principle: behavior at failure boundaries is the most important metric for earned autonomy.

**Sunny (2025) — Interactive Counterfactual Explanations for Trust Calibration**

Achieved highest trust alignment (rho=0.82) with lowest calibration error (CE=0.06) using interactive counterfactual explanations in loan approval simulations.

**Henrique et al. (2025) — Dynamic Trust Calibration via Contextual Bandits**

Showed 10-38% increases in task rewards and consistent trust misalignment reductions through adaptive, real-time trust calibration.

### Research Validation Summary

| Qorpera Design Decision | Research Support |
|---|---|
| Three-stage graduated autonomy | Matches Endsley's intermediate automation findings; mirrors AWS/McKinsey/Deloitte staged deployment models; aligns with Knight Columbia five-level framework |
| Per-situation-type graduation | Validated by Earned Autonomy Gradient's contextual autonomy principle and domain-specific trust calibration research |
| Accuracy-based graduation thresholds | Supported by Hoff & Bashir's learned trust layer and the Earned Autonomy Gradient's measurable, behavior-based trust |
| Evidence-based reasoning display | Validated by Zhang et al. confidence calibration research, tempered by Bansal et al.'s warning about undiscriminating acceptance |
| Autonomy demotion on performance degradation | Supported by Earned Autonomy Gradient's revocability principle and Lee & See's dynamic trust feedback loop |
| Human approval at Propose stage | Strongest anti-complacency mechanism; validated by Endsley's intermediate automation superiority and Bucinca's cognitive forcing research |

**Critical design risks to address:**
1. **Rubber-stamping at Propose stage** — the single biggest threat, requiring cognitive forcing interventions
2. **Transparency overload** — keep explanations focused (2-3 key signals), avoid comprehensive reasoning dumps
3. **Complacency at Act stage** — maintain visibility of autonomous actions and periodic human review
4. **Trust repair after errors** — implement immediate, specific, causal explanations when the AI is wrong
5. **The Bansal trap** — explanations that make wrong answers look right; separate evidence from recommendations
